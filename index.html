01
The Observer's Role
WMO-No.8 §1.1 · WMO-No.49 Vol I Part I Reg 1
Surface weather observations provide the primary data for numerical weather prediction (NWP), aviation safety, climate records, and the Global Telecommunication System (GTS).
Main observation elements
  • Air temperature and dew point / relative humidity
  • Wind direction and speed (10-min mean)
  • Visibility (MOR — Meteorological Optical Range)
  • Present and past weather (ww, W1, W2)
  • Cloud amount (N), type (CL, CM, CH) and base height (h)
  • Atmospheric pressure at station level and MSL
  • Precipitation amount and type
Synoptic observation hours (UTC)
TypeTimes (UTC)
Main synoptic00 · 06 · 12 · 18
Intermediate synoptic03 · 09 · 15 · 21
Aviation (METAR)Hourly (or 30-min at busy aerodromes)
SPECIWhen significant change occurs (any time)
Timeliness is mandatory. WMO-No.49 requires observations to be transmitted within 15 minutes of the main synoptic hour. A late or missing observation cannot be reconstructed — it is simply lost from the global record.
02
Temperature
WMO-No.8 Ch.2 · WMO-No.306 FM-12 group 1SnTTT
Stevenson Screen requirements (WMO-No.8 §2.2)
  • Height of thermometer bulbs: 1.25 m above ground level
  • Screen painted white, double-louvred, door facing away from equator (north in Southern Hemisphere, south in Northern — TNCA/TNCB/TNCC face north as they are in NH)
  • Sited on short natural grass; no artificial surfaces directly below
  • Minimum distance from obstructions: ≥ 4× the height of that obstruction
Accuracy and resolution
  • Uncertainty goal: ±0.3°C (WMO-No.8 Table 2.1)
  • Resolution: 0.1°C
  • Maximum temperature: read at 18:00 LST (or observation hour), reset immediately after
  • Minimum temperature: read at 06:00 LST (or observation hour), reset immediately after

03
Humidity & Dew Point
WMO-No.8 Ch.4 · FM-12 group 2SnTdTdTd
Dew point temperature (Td) is the temperature to which air must be cooled at constant pressure for saturation to occur (RH = 100%). It is always ≤ air temperature.
Psychrometer method (WMO-No.8 §4.2)
  • Dry-bulb (T): measures air temperature directly
  • Wet-bulb (Tw): covered with muslin wick kept moist with distilled water; cooling from evaporation lowers reading
  • Depression = T − Tw; enter psychrometric tables with T and depression to obtain Td and RH
  • Wick must be changed every 2–4 weeks; always use distilled or de-ionised water
Key relationships
  • T = Td → RH = 100% (fog / saturation possible)
  • T − Td < 3°C → high humidity, fog risk
  • T − Td > 15°C → very dry air
  • Typical Curaçao: Td ≈ 22–26°C, RH ≈ 75–85%
SYNOP encoding — group 2SnTdTdTd
Same encoding as temperature: prefix 2, then Sn (0=positive, 1=negative), then |Td|×10 in 3 digits.
Example: Td = 26.7°C → 20267  |  Td = −1.2°C → 21012
Alternative: if only RH available, use group 29UUU (U = RH in %).
04
Wind
WMO-No.8 Ch.5 · WMO-No.49 Reg 12.3 · FM-12 Nddff
Wind direction is reported as the direction from which the wind blows, in degrees true (not magnetic), measured clockwise from 000° (north). A southerly wind blows from the south → 180°.
Averaging periods (WMO-No.8 §5.8)
  • SYNOP: 10-minute mean speed and direction at observation time
  • METAR: 2-minute mean speed; gusts = maximum 3-second mean during the preceding 10 minutes
  • Gust reported when max 3-sec wind ≥ mean + 5 kt (or ≥ 15 kt above mean)
Measurement standard
  • Height: 10 m above the surrounding terrain (WMO-No.8 §5.9)
  • Exposure: no obstacles within 150 m upwind; obstacle height < 1/10 of distance
  • 1 m/s = 1.944 kt  |  1 kt = 0.514 m/s
Special cases
  • Calm: speed < 0.3 m/s (< 1 kt) → dd=00, ff=00 in SYNOP; CALM in METAR
  • Variable: no predominant direction → dd=99 in SYNOP; VRB in METAR
  • Speed ≥ 99 units: ff=99 in Nddff group, followed by 00fff extension with 3-digit speed
SYNOP encoding (dd and ff)
dd = direction ÷ 10 (round to nearest 10°, then ÷ 10). Example: 100° → dd = 10. ff = speed in m/s or kn (iw determines units).

Visibility
WMO-No.8 Ch.9 · FM-12 VV · FM-15 (METAR)
Meteorological Optical Range (MOR) — the length of path in the atmosphere required to reduce the luminous flux of a beam from an incandescent lamp at 2700 K to 5% of its original value (Koschmieder's Law). MOR defines visibility independent of day/night.
Prevailing visibility
The greatest visibility observed over at least half the horizon circle. For SYNOP, report this in the VV code. For METAR, report the prevailing visibility in metres (≥10 km → 9999).
Observation method
  • Establish a set of reference objects (landmarks, lights) at documented distances around the station
  • During daytime: dark objects against sky background
  • Night-time: lights of known intensity; reduce daytime visibility estimate by ~10%
  • Ceilometer/transmissometer gives automated MOR readings

Atmospheric Pressure
WMO-No.8 Ch.3 · FM-12 groups 3PPPP, 4PPPP, 5appp
Pressure definitions
TermDefinition
QFEActual pressure at the aerodrome/instrument level. Aircraft altimeter reads 0 ft at aerodrome elevation when set to QFE.
QNHICAO altimeter setting: pressure reduced to sea level using ISA (standard atmosphere 15°C, −6.5°C/km lapse). Aircraft altimeter reads aerodrome elevation when set to QNH. Used in METAR.
QFF (MSL)Station pressure reduced to sea level using the actual mean temperature of the air column. More accurate than QNH for synoptic analysis. Used in SYNOP group 4PPPP.
Standard atmosphere: 1013.25 hPa, 15°C at MSL. The mean sea-level pressure for synoptic analysis (isobars on weather maps) uses QFF, not QNH.
Accuracy requirements (WMO-No.8 §3.11)
  • Uncertainty: ±0.1 hPa
  • Resolution: 0.1 hPa
  • Barometers calibrated against primary standard; verified at each observation

Diurnal pressure oscillation (tropical)
At low-latitude stations (Curaçao, Aruba, Bonaire) pressure follows a consistent diurnal cycle: two maxima at ~10:00 and ~22:00 LST, two minima at ~04:00 and ~16:00 LST, amplitude ≈ ±1 hPa. Deviations from this pattern can indicate approaching disturbances.

Precipitation Measurement
WMO-No.8 Ch.6 · FM-12 group 6RRRt
Gauge types (WMO-No.8 §6.2)
  • Standard storage gauge: cylindrical receiver, 200 mm diameter; read with measuring cylinder graduated to 0.1 mm; at each synoptic hour
  • Tipping-bucket gauge: automated; each tip = 0.1 mm (or 0.2 mm); calibration essential — heavier rainfall rates may cause under-catch
  • Weighing gauge: continuous; can measure snow and solid precipitation

Station Exposure & Inspection
WMO-No.8 §1.3–1.5 · WMO-No.49 Appendix 1.B
The quality of any observation is directly limited by the quality of instrument exposure. A perfect instrument in a bad location gives a bad observation.
Temperature / humidity screen
  • Height: thermometer bulbs at 1.25 m AGL
  • Surface: short natural grass (not concrete, sand, or gravel)
  • Clearance: no obstruction within a horizontal distance of 4× its height
  • Screen: white paint maintained; hinges and latches in working order; aspirated where natural ventilation is insufficient
Wind anemometer
  • Height: 10 m AGL on a mast
  • Fetch: no obstacle upwind closer than 10× its height for the prevailing wind direction; no obstacle taller than 1/10 of its distance
  • At aerodromes: representative of the runway threshold / middle
Precipitation gauge
  • Rim height: 30 cm above ground
  • Distance from nearest obstacle: ≥ 2× its height
  • Level: gauge must be truly vertical (use spirit level)
Visibility
  • Observer position: unobstructed horizontal view over ≥ 180° of the horizon
  • Reference points: documented list of objects/lights with known azimuths and distances
  • Automated transmissometer: path length typically 75 m; needs calibration verification
Daily inspection checklist
  • Check all instrument readings are within expected range
  • Inspect Stevenson screen: clean louvers, fresh wick on wet-bulb
  • Empty and measure rain gauge; check tipping-bucket mechanism
  • Verify anemometer rotation (cup type); check data logger time
  • Confirm barometer zero correction is applied; compare with backup barometer
  • Record any anomalies, maintenance, or site changes in the station log
